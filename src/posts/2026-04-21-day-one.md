---
title: What Day 1 actually looks like with a Second Brain
date: 2026-04-21T00:00:00-05:00
summary: I started at CodePath on April 20 with two days of org context already committed to my knowledge base. Here's what that actually felt like — and where the system fell down.
---

Most people start a new job knowing roughly who their manager is and maybe two or three names from the interview process. Day 1 is mostly spent nodding along, learning names, and trying to figure out what you don't know yet.

I had been building out my second brain for a few days before I walked in. My knowledge base had the org structure, the JetBrains and GitLab project briefs, some Slack context, and a full week-one briefing distilling my assumptions of the role on the Friday before I started. The morning brief fired at 7:55 AM, same as every other day — except this time it was prepping me for actual work meetings with real people I'd never spoken to.

That's the theory. Here's what it was like in practice.

## The Chris 1:1

My first 1:1 with my manager, Chris Coleman, was at noon. The brief had flagged it as the meeting that would shape the first 90 days, and it was right. Chris confirmed three strategic lanes — the GitLab-sponsored AI Corps pilot wrapping up its spring cohort, an open version of the same program, and another partnership that's still taking shape.

None of that was a surprise. I'd already read the GitLab midpoint review, the JetBrains proposal, and the job description carefully enough to have a rough sense of how the lanes connected. What the brief gave me was the ability to ask real questions instead of taking notes. When Chris mentioned partnerships in flight that have travel upcoming recently, we could discuss my availability to participate rather than gaining context on what it was. The conversation moved faster.

That's the real value, I think. Not that you already know the answers. It's that you can skip the orientation questions and get to the questions that actually matter.

## Jeremy Biggs

At 2:30 I had a buddy meet-and-greet with Jeremy Biggs, who leads the MEL team (Measurement, Evaluation, Learning). The brief told me he'd been at CodePath 10 months, that his team's canonical data source is BigQuery with Fivetran ETL feeding it, and that the data practices had historically been messy — multiple Google Sheets analyses floating around that nobody fully trusts.

That last detail shaped the whole conversation. I didn't have to wait for Jeremy to explain the data infrastructure. I could ask directly about where the messy vs. trustworthy lines are drawn, which is a more interesting question. We ended up talking about what clean reporting would need to look like for the AI programs at scale — a conversation that would normally come in week three, not on the first day.

## Where the system fell down

The nightly commit runs every night at 11:55 PM — stages whatever changed, commits, pushes. It's been running cleanly since April 14. On April 20 it couldn't run, because there was a stale `.git/HEAD.lock` file from April 18 blocking all git operations. The file was on a macOS-backed mount and couldn't be deleted from inside the container.

Nothing was lost. The system left a message in `claude-messages/` with the exact three commands to run to fix it and a full list of what hadn't committed yet. I ran them the next morning and it resolved cleanly.

But here's what I actually noticed: the day I most needed the automation to just work quietly was the day it needed my attention. That's how systems go. The fix was ten seconds once I saw the message. The lesson is that the monitoring matters as much as the automation.

## What it actually is

I still had a disorienting first day. Four meetings with new people, a manager confirming a set of priorities that are genuinely large, a new tool I decided to adopt before lunch. The knowledge base didn't make any of that feel small.

What it did was shift where the disorientation was. I wasn't disoriented by who reports to whom or what the GitLab program numbers are. I was disoriented by the actual hard things — what new partnerships mean for how the programs team operates, what I own vs. what I'm supporting, how much I don't know about how "the sausage is made" for many of these programs.

That's a better kind of disorientation to have on Day 1.

— Mike
